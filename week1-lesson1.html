Reading: The Road to Federation (approx. 1100 words)

In the late 19th century, Australia was not one country but six separate British colonies: New South Wales, Victoria, Queensland, South Australia, Western Australia, and Tasmania. Each colony had its own parliament, laws, and even its own defence forces and postal systems. There was growing recognition that this arrangement was inefficient and left the colonies vulnerable to external threats and economic challenges.

The push for federation gained real momentum in the 1880s and 1890s. One of the most important figures was Sir Henry Parkes, five-time Premier of New South Wales. On 24 October 1889, Parkes delivered the famous Tenterfield Oration in Tenterfield, New South Wales. In this speech, he argued passionately for the colonies to unite under “one great national government for all Australia.” He highlighted the need for a united defence force and a single national approach to trade and immigration. The speech is widely regarded as the spark that reignited serious public discussion about federation.

Following the Tenterfield Oration, colonial leaders organised the Australasian Federation Conference in Melbourne in 1890. Delegates agreed that a national convention should be held to draft a constitution. The first National Australasian Convention met in Sydney in 1891. Key figures included Edmund Barton (who would become Australia’s first Prime Minister), Alfred Deakin (a leading Victorian politician and later Prime Minister), and Sir Samuel Griffith (Premier of Queensland and later Chief Justice of the High Court). These men, along with others such as Andrew Inglis Clark from Tasmania, played central roles in shaping the draft constitution.

The 1891 draft was not immediately adopted due to economic depression and political disagreements. However, popular support continued to grow. In 1897–1898, a second series of conventions was held. The final draft constitution was put to referendums in the colonies between 1898 and 1900. After successful referendums (with some amendments requested by the British government), the *Commonwealth of Australia Constitution Act 1900* was passed by the United Kingdom Parliament. On 1 January 1901, the Commonwealth of Australia came into existence.

The Constitution established a federal system in which power is divided between the national (Commonwealth) government and the state governments. It created a bicameral Parliament (House of Representatives and Senate), an Executive Government led by the Prime Minister, and an independent Judiciary headed by the High Court. This hybrid system — often called the 'Washminster system' — combines responsible government from the British Westminster tradition with a federal structure and written constitution influenced by the United States.

Primary source records held by the National Archives of Australia document the conventions, the referendums, and the final passage of the Constitution Act. These records show the debates over key issues such as the powers of the Senate, the division of financial resources, and the protection of state interests. The Constitution remains Australia’s foundational legal document and continues to shape how power is exercised today.
