Reading: The Road to Federation

In the late 19th century, Australia was not one country but six separate British colonies: New South Wales, Victoria, Queensland, South Australia, Western Australia, and Tasmania. Each colony had its own parliament, laws, and even its own defence forces and postal systems. There was growing recognition that this arrangement was inefficient and left the colonies vulnerable.

The push for federation gained real momentum in the 1880s and 1890s. One of the most important figures was Sir Henry Parkes, five-time Premier of New South Wales. On 24 October 1889, Parkes delivered the famous Tenterfield Oration. He argued passionately for the colonies to unite under “one great national government for all Australia.”

Following this, colonial leaders organised the Australasian Federation Conference in Melbourne in 1890. The first National Australasian Convention met in Sydney in 1891. Key figures included Edmund Barton, Alfred Deakin, and Sir Samuel Griffith. These men played central roles in shaping the draft constitution.

The final draft was put to referendums in the colonies. After successful referendums, the Commonwealth of Australia Constitution Act 1900 was passed. On 1 January 1901, the Commonwealth of Australia came into existence.

The Constitution established a federal system with power divided between the Commonwealth and the states. It created a bicameral Parliament, an Executive Government, and an independent Judiciary. This hybrid system is often called the 'Washminster' system.
